Coco Soboo Moto biography

The Rastafarian radio presenter is second born in a family of four. He is the only boy. From the middle name, it is assumed that Coco Sobo Moto tribe is Kikuyu. His family has spent much of its time moving between Kenya and South Africa, occasionally travelling to other parts of the world due to the nature of his parents' work.

Coco Soboo Moto childhood

The media star admits he was not an easy child growing up. JahSeed grew surrounded with abundance only to turn out a spoilt child in his mid-teen years.

At age 14, Coco accidentally found himself in the middle of drug abuse. He learned the habit of a man who sold ice cream around his school in Kenya. Clueless, Coco took his heroin laced ice cream and only became an addict by day.

Coco Soboo Moto education

Upon completing his primary education in Kenya, Coco relocated to South Africa, along with his parents. He moved to Australia for further education at the college level. Coco regularly missed classes for as long as three weeks.

His enrollment ended with almost an expulsion because the institution could no longer tolerate his absenteeism and performance. He decided to quit. Coco lately feels that he wasted his youthful years and is now going the extra mile in his career to recover all his lost years.

Struggles with heroin

What started as a simple ice cream flavouring eventually ended in a full blast addiction. Coco would sell off random household items to meet his rising addiction demands. At the time, he did not realize he was getting off track because substance abuse was a new thing in his life.

Coco Sobo Moto car accidents followed when his substance abuse habit escalated. He recalls surviving nine gristly car crashes, where the cars were damaged beyond repair, but he always came out alive. Unfortunately, these fatal experiences did little to break his substance abuse.

Blacking out on the streets became a part of his life gradually and left him without a future.

Rehabilitation and journey from addiction

The reggae star went into rehabilitation about 20 times before beating his addiction. He only became serious when his parents threatened to withdraw financial support. Kariuki has been in recovery for over 17 years, which involved going to Mathare North Health Centre for the daily dose of methadone. This is a substitute drug for heroin that reduces withdrawal symptoms.
